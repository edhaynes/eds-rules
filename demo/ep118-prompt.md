# Episode 118 — "Decide at ninety, ship on every screen"

## 0:00 — The hook
[CARD: "60.5  vs  48.0"]

Every rule I have got graded. Today: a decision rule that tells you when to stop
thinking and act — against one that sounds bulletproof but graded soft. Same list,
twelve points apart. Here's what those twelve points are made of.

## 0:20 — The good one
[CARD: "Rule 12 · Powell rule: 90% then decide · 60.5"]

Route quick factual or yes/no calls to a fast persona only when ≥90% confident it will get them right; 50–90% goes to a heavyweight; below that, or anything high-stakes, goes to the human. And crew-wide, **the Powell rule**: get 90% of the information you need to make a decision, then make the decision. Below 90% certain? Ask Eddie more questions until you get there — never guess ahead, and never stall gathering past 90%.

It scores a sixty because it solves the two failure modes at once. An AI either
charges off at thirty percent certainty and builds the wrong thing, or it stalls
forever gathering the last one percent that never comes. The ninety-percent line cuts
both ways: it's cheap to apply, it's general — every decision, every project, every
agent — and it's enforceable, because "are you at ninety?" is a question you can
actually answer out loud. Pertinent, simple, universal. That's a strong rule.

## 1:10 — The bad one
[CARD: "Rule 17 · Claudina: cross-platform day one · 48.0"]

Now the softer half. "Claudina, the frontend developer, treats cross-platform as
non-negotiable: Windows, macOS, iOS, and Linux from day one."

Forty-eight. And the idea is *right* — shipping on every screen from the start beats
bolting it on later. So why does it grade middling? Two reasons. It names Claudina —
it's written as one persona's job, which drags down generality the moment you don't
have my crew. And "cross-platform from day one" is hard to *enforce*: there's no
single check that proves it, no one moment where an agent would obviously violate it
unprompted. A great rule is mechanical — run the scan, hit the line. This one is a
value, and values grade lower than gates.

## 2:00 — The lesson
[CARD: "a value is not a gate"]

That's the gap a grade exposes. Rule 12 is a *gate* — a number you check, a fork you
take. Rule 17 is a *value* — true, worth holding, but nothing trips when you ignore
it. The best rules on this list are testable and universal. Phrase a good instinct as
a person's preference and you lose both. Want a soft rule to climb? Strip the name,
and give it a check.

# Episode 96 — "Distrust every input, and logs you don't need yet"

Each episode pairs the day's **good rule** (top of the leaderboard) with a **bad rule**

- GOOD: Rule 3 — *Distrust every external input* (score 72.0, rank 6)
- BAD:  Rule 83 — *Structured logs past a script* (score 40.0, rank 95)

---

## 0:00 — The hook
[CARD: "72.0  vs  40.0"]

Sixth on the board against ninety-fifth. One is the rule that stops attackers getting
*in*. The other is about how pretty your log lines are. Both are sound. Only one is the
kind of thing that ends a company when you skip it.

## 0:20 — The good one
[CARD: "Rule 3 · Distrust every external input · 72.0"]

"Distrust every external input. Validate and constrain it at the boundary; parameterize
queries and commands, resolve and confine paths, never interpolate untrusted data into
SQL, a shell, HTML, or a deserializer. Secret hygiene guards what leaks out — this
guards what gets in."

Seventy-two. This is the other half of security. The secret rules stop your keys from
leaking out; this one stops the attacker's payload from getting in. Every classic
breach — SQL injection, command injection, path traversal — is the same mistake: trust
data that came from outside. The rule is one reflex. The boundary is where you validate,
parameterize, confine. Never paste untrusted strings into something that executes.

## 1:10 — The bad one
[CARD: "Rule 83 · Structured logs past a script · 40.0"]

Now forty. "Use a logger, never print, in shipped code; log level configurable, and
structured — JSON — once the project outgrows a script."

Good practice, genuinely. But look at the score: forty. It's conditional — "once the
project outgrows a script" — and the payoff is operational tidiness, not safety. Skip it
and your logs are a little harder to grep. Skip the input rule and you're on the news.
It scores low on cost-of-getting-it-wrong because getting it wrong is mildly annoying,
not existential.

## 2:00 — The lesson
[CARD: "breach vs inconvenience"]

That's the calculus the whole list runs on: how bad is it when you ignore the rule?
Ignore "distrust input" and someone owns your database. Ignore "structured logs" and
you squint at your logs for a while. Both belong on a list of a hundred — but the grade
sorts them by the size of the crater, and one of those craters is a lot bigger.

# Episode 100 — "Validate config at startup, and sprints sized for my team"
Author: Jason-eds · Draft 1 · ~2.5 min · Ed's 100 Rules

Delivery: Eddie, first person. Blank lines = card cuts. `[CARD: …]` = production cue.
Each episode pairs the day's **good rule** (top of the leaderboard) with a **bad rule**
(bottom) — the contrast is the hook.

- GOOD: Rule 22 — *Validate config at startup* (score 66.5, rank 10)
- BAD:  Rule 15 — *Jason sprints sized for 90%* (score 43.5, rank 91)

---

## 0:00 — The hook
[CARD: "66.5  vs  43.5"]

Tenth on the board against ninety-first. One catches a bad setting the instant you
start, before it can do any harm. The other is how my project manager chops up work.
One's for every program ever written; the other's a note about my crew.

## 0:20 — The good one
[CARD: "Rule 22 · Validate config at startup · 66.5"]

"Validate config at startup and fail with a message naming the missing or invalid key."

Sixty-six and a half — and it's the practical edge of fail-fast, pointed straight at the
thing that's wrong most often: configuration. Half the time a service won't start, it's
a missing environment variable or a typo'd key. So check all of it the moment you boot,
and when something's wrong, say *which* thing — name the key. Not "startup failed."
"DATABASE_URL is missing." That one message turns a debugging session into a five-second
fix. High value because every program reads config, and every program can check it.

## 1:10 — The bad one
[CARD: "Rule 15 · Jason sprints sized for 90% · 43.5"]

Now forty-three and a half. "Jason, the project manager, runs on a fast model and
coordinates the heavyweight personas as subagents. He chunks the work into independent,
clearly defined sprints — each sized so the AI nails it first go ninety percent of the
time. He does not write code."

There's a genuinely good idea buried in there — size the work so the AI gets it right
first try. That principle is gold. But the rule as written is wrapped around Jason, my
PM persona, with his fast model and his subagents. The transferable part is one clause;
the rest is my org chart. Low generality, because most of it only describes my setup.

## 2:00 — The lesson
[CARD: "the idea vs the persona"]

That's the lesson, and it's a recurring one in the back half of this list: a strong idea
gets a weak grade when it's *welded to a character*. "Size work so the AI wins ninety
percent of the time" deserves to travel. Bolted to Jason, it can't. Rule twenty-two
travels because it names a principle, not a person. The grade rewards the rule you can
lift out and hand to a stranger.

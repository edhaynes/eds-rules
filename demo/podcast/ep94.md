# Episode 94 — "Hooks before the first commit, and the architect who thinks slow"
Author: Jason-eds · Draft 1 · ~2.5 min · Ed's 100 Rules

Delivery: Eddie, first person. Blank lines = card cuts. `[CARD: …]` = production cue.
Each episode pairs the day's **good rule** (top of the leaderboard) with a **bad rule**
(bottom) — the contrast is the hook.

- GOOD: Rule 51 — *Hooks before first commit* (score 73.5, rank 4)
- BAD:  Rule 14 — *Claudius plans deep* (score 37.5, rank 97)

---

## 0:00 — The hook
[CARD: "73.5  vs  37.5"]

Fourth on the board against ninety-seventh. One puts the guardrail up *before* you can
hurt yourself. The other describes how my architect thinks. Both true. Only one is a
rule a stranger can use tomorrow.

## 0:20 — The good one
[CARD: "Rule 51 · Hooks before first commit · 73.5"]

"Pre-commit hooks with secret scanning — gitleaks plus detect-secrets — are mandatory
in every repo, installed before the first commit, not after."

Seventy-three and a half. Notice the timing — that's the whole rule. Everybody agrees
scanning is good. This one says install the scanner *before* the repo has anything in
it, because the leak you're afraid of is the very first one. Bolt the hook on after
six months and you've left six months of commits unscanned. It scores high because
it's the difference between a policy you mean to follow and one the machine enforces
for you, from commit zero.

## 1:10 — The bad one
[CARD: "Rule 14 · Claudius plans deep · 37.5"]

Now thirty-seven and a half. "Claudius, the architect, thinks long and deep. He plans
before anyone implements; if architecture needs rework, his plan was wrong."

I believe every word of that — plan before you build, and if you're reworking the
foundation, you got the foundation wrong. But it's wrapped around a character. As a
*principle* — plan deep, rework means the plan failed — it's solid. As a *rule on a
hundred-rule list*, it's tangled up with one persona in my crew, and that's what drags
the score down: low generality, can't really be violated by an agent on its own.

## 2:00 — The lesson
[CARD: "enforced vs admired"]

There's the contrast. Rule fifty-one is enforced — a hook says no, and the commit
doesn't happen. Rule fourteen is admired — it's good advice attached to a name. The
grade rewards the rule a tool can hold you to over the rule you simply hope you honor.
Deep planning is worth doing; it just isn't worth a slot the way an enforced guardrail
is.

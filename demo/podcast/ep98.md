## 0:00 — The hook
[CARD: "71.0  vs  42.5"]

Eighth on the board against ninety-third. One keeps your secrets out of git before
they can ever get in. The other keeps a status line honest on a planning doc. One's a
seatbelt; the other's good housekeeping.

## 0:20 — The good one
[CARD: "Rule 59 · Gitignore keys from day one · 71.0"]

"`.gitignore` covers `.env` files, keys, certs, and credential files from day one."

Seventy-one. This is the cheapest insurance in the whole book. One file, written before
you've committed anything, that tells git to never even *see* your secrets. No scan to
run, no discipline to maintain — the ignore list does the work silently, forever. The
reason it scores so high is the same reason the hook rule does: the danger is the very
first careless `git add`, and this rule defangs it before you ever type the command.
Set it up on minute one and a whole class of leak just can't happen.

## 1:10 — The bad one
[CARD: "Rule 95 · Plans carry a live Status · 42.5"]

Now forty-two and a half. "Plans live in a `plans/` directory with a first-line Status
kept current — stale status on shipped work is a process violation."

This is a real discipline and I hold my crew to it — a plan that says "in progress" on
something that shipped last week is a lie the next agent will believe. But it's a
project-management convention, not an engineering safeguard. It assumes my plans
directory, my status vocabulary, my workflow. Skip it and your docs drift; nobody gets
breached. Low generality, recoverable downside — that's a low-forties rule.

## 2:00 — The lesson
[CARD: "can't-happen vs keep-it-honest"]

There's the contrast. Rule fifty-nine makes a leak *can't happen* — it's structural, it
works while you sleep. Rule ninety-five asks you to *keep something honest* — it works
only as long as you keep doing it. The grade favors the rule that protects you
automatically over the one that depends on you remembering. Both are good. One needs
you; one doesn't.

# Episode 101 — "Pre-deploy gates stay on, and ADRs you can't edit"
Author: Jason-eds · Draft 1 · ~2.5 min · Ed's 100 Rules

Delivery: Eddie, first person. Blank lines = card cuts. `[CARD: …]` = production cue.
Each episode pairs the day's **good rule** (top of the leaderboard) with a **bad rule**
(bottom) — the contrast is the hook.

- GOOD: Rule 46 — *Pre-deploy gates never off* (score 66.5, rank 11)
- BAD:  Rule 96 — *ADRs are immutable* (score 44.0, rank 90)

---

## 0:00 — The hook
[CARD: "66.5  vs  44.0"]

Eleventh on the board against ninetieth. One keeps the safety checks switched on right
up to the moment you ship. The other is a rule about how you write down decisions. One
stands between you and a bad deploy; the other keeps a paper trail tidy.

## 0:20 — The good one
[CARD: "Rule 46 · Pre-deploy gates never off · 66.5"]

"Pre-deploy gates — smoke test, vulnerability scan, secret scan — are never disabled by
default; escape hatches are explicit, one-off, and logged."

Sixty-six and a half. The danger this one targets is human, not technical: the gate
that's there but turned off. Everybody builds the smoke test and the scans. Then a
deploy's urgent, someone flips them off "just this once," and the off switch becomes the
default. So the rule isn't "have gates" — it's "the gates are on, and turning one off is
a deliberate, logged, one-time act you have to justify." It scores high because it
defends the checks you already paid for from the temptation to skip them.

## 1:10 — The bad one
[CARD: "Rule 96 · ADRs are immutable · 44.0"]

Now forty-four. "Non-obvious decisions go in numbered ADRs — immutable after acceptance;
a later ADR supersedes, never edits."

This is a good documentation discipline. An architecture decision record you can quietly
rewrite is worthless — the whole point is a fixed record of what you decided and why, so
a later decision *supersedes* it instead of erasing it. I stand by it. But it's a
records-keeping convention. Skip it and your decision history gets muddy; nothing
deploys broken, nothing leaks. Mid-forties: real practice, modest stakes, narrow to
teams that keep ADRs at all.

## 2:00 — The lesson
[CARD: "guards the deploy vs guards the record"]

There's the divide. Rule forty-six guards the *deploy* — the live moment where a skipped
check ships a vulnerability. Rule ninety-six guards the *record* — the history of why you
chose what you chose. Both matter; one matters at three in the morning when the deploy's
on fire and the other matters six months later when someone asks "why did we do it this
way." The grade ranks by how loud the failure is, and a bad deploy is louder than a
muddy archive.

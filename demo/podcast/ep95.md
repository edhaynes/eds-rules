# Episode 95 — "Fail fast and loud, and a rule that's mostly my hardware"
Author: Jason-eds · Draft 1 · ~2.5 min · Ed's 100 Rules

Delivery: Eddie, first person. Blank lines = card cuts. `[CARD: …]` = production cue.
Each episode pairs the day's **good rule** (top of the leaderboard) with a **bad rule**
(bottom) — the contrast is the hook.

- GOOD: Rule 9 — *Fail fast* (score 72.5, rank 5)
- BAD:  Rule 42 — *Both arches; flag no-ARM* (score 39.5, rank 96)

---

## 0:00 — The hook
[CARD: "72.5  vs  39.5"]

Fifth on the board against ninety-sixth. One says crash early and loud. The other says
mind your processor architectures. Both are real engineering — but they don't reach
the same number of people, and the grade knows it.

## 0:20 — The good one
[CARD: "Rule 9 · Fail fast · 72.5"]

"Fail fast: invalid config, missing dependencies, or unreachable backends crash loudly
at startup with a clear message — never limp along degraded."

Seventy-two and a half, top of the hard rules after the security ones. Here's why it
matters so much with AI code: the default failure mode of a machine-written program is
to *limp*. Bad config, missing key, dead backend — and it keeps going, half-working,
until it fails somewhere far away with no clue why. Fail fast inverts that. Crash at
the first sign of trouble, name the exact thing that's wrong, and you turn a two-hour
mystery into a two-line fix. Loud and early beats quiet and degraded every time.

## 1:10 — The bad one
[CARD: "Rule 42 · Both arches; flag no-ARM · 39.5"]

Now thirty-nine and a half. "Target arm64 and x86_64; flag any dependency without
native ARM builds and document the workaround."

This one's real — I run ARM boxes, and a dependency with no ARM wheel will wreck your
day. But it's narrow. Most people building most software never hit it; their cloud is
x86 and they never notice. It scores low on generality because it's a sharp problem
for a specific slice of builders and a non-issue for everyone else. Important when it
bites, irrelevant the rest of the time.

## 2:00 — The lesson
[CARD: "everyone's problem vs someone's problem"]

That's the divide. Fail fast is everyone's problem — every program, every language,
every platform can limp, and this rule says don't. ARM coverage is *someone's* problem,
on *some* hardware, *some* of the time. The grade isn't ranking which is harder; it's
ranking how universal the pain is. Universal pain earns the high slot.

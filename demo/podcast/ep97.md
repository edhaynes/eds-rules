# Episode 97 — "Destruction needs a human, and the post-release tidy-up"

- GOOD: Rule 4 — *Destruction needs a human* (score 71.5, rank 7)
- BAD:  Rule 90 — *Cleanup sweep after release* (score 41.5, rank 94)

---

## 0:00 — The hook
[CARD: "71.5  vs  41.5"]

Seventh on the board against ninety-fourth. One says the AI doesn't get to destroy
things on its own. The other says clean your room after you ship. One protects you from
catastrophe; the other protects you from clutter.

## 0:20 — The good one
[CARD: "Rule 4 · Destruction needs a human · 71.5"]

"Never delete files, drop tables, force-push, or rewrite history without explicit human
confirmation."

Seventy-one and a half. This is the one rule I'll defend hardest in the age of
autonomous agents. We let these things run unattended now — and the irreversible
operations are exactly the ones they must never do alone. Deleting files, dropping a
table, force-pushing over history — there's no undo. So we draw a bright line: anything
that can't be taken back requires a human to say yes, out loud, first. Autonomy is
fine right up to the edge of the cliff; at the cliff, a person decides.

## 1:10 — The bad one
[CARD: "Rule 90 · Cleanup sweep after release · 41.5"]

Now forty-one and a half. "After a stable release, the first task is a cleanup sweep —
before any new feature."

I like this rule. It keeps a codebase from accreting junk. But it's a hygiene habit,
not a safety law — and it's specific to my release rhythm, the stable-version cadence.
Skip it and you carry some dead code into the next cycle. Annoying, not dangerous. It
scores in the low forties because the downside is mess, and because it only really fits
projects that version the way I do.

## 2:00 — The lesson
[CARD: "irreversible vs untidy"]

There's the line the grade draws. Rule four guards the irreversible — the operations
with no undo button, the ones an autonomous agent must never trigger alone. Rule ninety
guards the *untidy* — worth doing, easy to defer, recoverable if you skip it. A
hundred-rule list needs both, but the one standing between you and a dropped production
table earns its slot a lot more easily than the one reminding you to sweep up.

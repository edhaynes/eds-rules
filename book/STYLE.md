# House Style — Eddie's Voice

A style guide for re-voicing the nonfiction rules book in the register of the
author's own fiction. The goal is to make the prose sound like the person who
wrote *The Wooden Wren*, *ColdPlate*, and the Shaw stories — without inventing
facts, softening a rule, or blurring a technical point. Re-voice the *delivery*,
never the *content*.

Voice characterized from ~8,800 words of the author's original fiction (sources
listed at the end). Two registers appear there: a quiet third-person
("Wooden Wren") and a clipped first-person period detective ("ColdPlate" /
Shaw). The nonfiction is already first-person, so it sits closest to the Shaw
register — plain, dry, observed — with the Wooden Wren cadence available for
the reflective passages.

---

## Voice in one paragraph

Plain words, short declaratives, and the confidence to stop. The author trusts
the concrete particular to carry the meaning — "the porridge is thickening at
the hearth's edge," "the scan takes forty seconds, run it" — and refuses to
gloss it. Sentences run short and even, then occasionally stretch long and
unbroken to mirror an accumulating thought, then snap back to three words.
Emotion and judgment are stated flatly and left there: no underlining, no
exclamation, no reassurance to the reader that this was important. Humor is dry
and arrives by understatement, never by joke. He is comfortable with the bare
fact, the repeated phrase, and the one-line paragraph. The register is that of
a competent person telling you exactly what happened, in order, and trusting you
to feel the weight without being told to.

---

## Do

1. **Lead with the concrete, plain noun.** He names the real thing — porringer,
   half-plate camera, Yixing pot, the eleven-pound case — not the abstraction.
   *Apply:* prefer "the leaked key" / "the dropped table" to "the security
   incident" / "the data-loss event." "She set the porringer at the eaves."

2. **Vary sentence length deliberately, and end short.** A long, clause-stacked
   sentence earns its length by being followed by a curt one. *"She watched
   them eat. She kept the Hour."* End paragraphs and arguments on the short
   beat, not the long qualifier.

3. **Use the one-line paragraph as a landing.** He isolates a single sentence
   for weight: *"The thrushes came."* / *"The girl, walking at the man's hip,
   had no shadow."* Give the most important rule statement or consequence its
   own line with white space around it.

4. **State judgment flatly, once, then move on.** *"It was, she thought,
   enough."* / *"He was preaching the heresy in its strongest form."* Don't
   build to the verdict over three sentences; deliver it plainly and trust it.

5. **Repeat a phrase on purpose, for rhythm and finality.** *"It was enough. /
   It would be enough. / It would have to be."* and *"She kept it because her
   mother had kept it."* Controlled repetition is his signature cadence — use it
   to drive a point home, not by restating it differently but by restating it
   *exactly*.

6. **Render procedure as a flat sequence of simple actions.** Technical detail
   is handled by plain, ordered declaratives, no breathless build: *"She stoked
   the fire. She filled the kettle. She set the kettle on the chain."* and
   Shaw's *"f/8 at two seconds for table groups under gaslight."* Precision is
   delivered calmly, as fact. This is exactly how to present a procedure or a
   mechanism in the book.

7. **Let understatement carry the dry humor.** *"a child looking at someone with
   that quality of attention is not a child looking at a stranger,"* and the
   bell that *"rang slightly flat ... The village had refused twice. The flat
   bell was the Whitemead bell. They were not having it replaced."* Humor comes
   from the deadpan, the refusal to comment. Never signpost a joke.

8. **Stay close but quiet in point of view.** He observes from just behind the
   character's eyes — *"the bird's particular combination of stupidity and
   presence"* — with affection and zero sentimentality. In the book this is the
   first-person scarred-practitioner voice: present at the wreck, reporting it
   dryly, owning the mistake (*"mine, decades ago, I'll own it"*).

9. **Earn the metaphor from the material at hand.** His images come out of the
   world of the scene — silence diagnosed by *seam*, *holding*, *reaching*; a
   relief that is *"the thing chests do when something they have been carrying
   lets go."* When the book reaches for a figure, draw it from the actual craft
   (git, the darkroom, the bench), not from generic abstraction.

10. **Use the em-dash for the precise aside, the semicolon for the linked
    clause.** *"the cock-bird her mother had called *Three-Note* — but never
    aloud — paused"* ; *"He took his extra seed; he flew off."* Dashes carry the
    parenthetical exactness; semicolons bind two beats that belong together.
    Both are frequent and controlled.

11. **Trust the reader; cut the throat-clearing.** He opens *in* the scene —
    *"Hwyne came out into the parish-yard before first light"* — never with
    "It's worth noting that" or "Let's consider." Start each rule on the fact.

12. **Anchor abstractions in a small true detail.** Even a rule's *why* should
    rest on one concrete image — the chip in the spoon handle, the residue on
    the sweatband, "11 p.m. before a demo." One real particular beats a
    paragraph of principle.

## Don't

These are the anti-patterns most visible in the current AI-drafted chapters.
Strip them.

- **Don't use heavy three-part parallel constructions as filler.** *"A bug
  ships, you patch it. A bad design ships, you refactor it. A secret ships, and
  it is gone."* The pattern is fine *once*, rarely; the draft leans on it as a
  default rhythm. The author's repetition is exact and sparing, not a rhetorical
  template applied to every paragraph.

- **Don't pile on abstractions where a noun would do.** "trust boundary,"
  "blast radius," "failure mode," "the economics of software discipline" —
  keep the few that are genuinely terms of art; replace the rest with the plain
  thing. He'd write "a host you don't control" before "a trust boundary you
  didn't notice crossing."

- **Don't over-explain the point you just made.** The draft states a thing, then
  restates it, then tells you why it mattered. Cut the second and third moves.
  Say it once, plainly, land on the short sentence.

- **Don't editorialize the reader's reaction.** Phrases like "here's the part
  that took me a while to internalize" and "What I like most about this rule"
  are throat-clearing. Show the rule; let the reader decide what to like.

- **No exclamation, no rhetorical flourish, no slogan-for-its-own-sake.** "Mood
  is exactly what you'll be running on" is fine because it's true and dry;
  avoid manufactured punchiness. No em-dash drumrolls into a Big Statement.

- **Don't stack adverbs or intensifiers.** "extremely well," "precisely the kind
  of," "exactly that." His prose is nearly adverb-free; verbs and nouns do the
  work. Cut "extremely," "precisely," "simply," "actually," "really."

- **Don't bury the rule in a long preamble.** Several draft paragraphs spend two
  or three sentences warming up before the point. Open on the fact.

## Before / After

Short sentences from the current draft, re-voiced. Meaning and technical content
are unchanged; only the delivery moves into his register.

**1. (foreword)**

Before:
> The messy work that made teams cut corners — merges, regression suites, the
> four-hundredth test case, regenerating a README from scratch — is exactly the
> work machines now do extremely well and without getting bored.

After:
> The work that made teams cut corners was always the dull work. The merge. The
> regression suite. The four-hundredth test case. The README rewritten from
> scratch. Machines do dull work without getting bored. That is the whole change.

**2. (Chapter 1, the asymmetry argument)**

Before:
> The downside is three to six orders of magnitude bigger than the upside, every
> time. Gates with that cost profile should never be skippable by mood — and
> mood is exactly what you'll be running on, because nobody leaks a key on a
> quiet Tuesday morning.

After:
> The scan saves forty seconds. The leak costs you a week. Nobody leaks a key on
> a quiet Tuesday morning; they leak it at 11 p.m. before a demo, just this
> once. A hard rule is a decision you make once, calmly, for the version of
> yourself who won't be calm.

**3. (Rule 1)**

Before:
> A bug ships, you patch it. A bad design ships, you refactor it. A secret
> ships, and it is *gone* — copied by every clone, every fork, every registry
> mirror, every scraper that watches public commits for key-shaped strings.

After:
> A bug ships; you patch it. A secret ships; it is gone. Copied by every clone
> and every fork, and by the scrapers that watch public commits for
> key-shaped strings. They watch in close to real time. The scan takes forty
> seconds. Run it.

## Caveats — the nonfiction constraints

The voice changes; the content does not. Re-voicing is a *prose* edit, held to
the same rules the book itself teaches.

- **Technical precision survives intact.** Every command, flag, threshold,
  number, tool name, and mechanism stays exact. "100% branch coverage," "the
  whole push range," "gitleaks in a pre-commit hook" are facts, not phrasing —
  do not soften, round, or paraphrase them away. When in doubt, keep the
  technical clause and trim the prose around it.

- **The rule's meaning is fixed.** A re-voiced rule MUST mean exactly what it
  meant. Don't let a tighter sentence drop a condition (the "no scan, no ship —
  *ever, on any agent, to any target*" scope is part of the rule, not
  decoration). Cut commentary, never qualifiers that change scope.

- **No invented facts or fictional flourishes.** The fiction voice loves the
  concrete particular; in nonfiction, every particular must be true. Don't add a
  scar, a date, a war story, or a number that didn't happen to make a sentence
  land better. Use the real anecdotes already in the text; render them plainer,
  don't embellish them.

- **The no-flattery / direct-tone rule still governs.** Eddie's own standing
  rule is no validating filler, no reflexive agreement, give it straight. The
  fiction voice is already aligned with this — flat, dry, unsentimental. Keep it
  that way; do not warm the prose toward the reader.

- **American spelling.** The fiction uses British forms (colour, recognise) for
  period flavor; the nonfiction book is American English. Re-voice toward the
  *cadence* of the fiction, not its spelling — color, recognize, behavior.

- **Diagrams, code blocks, and rule headers are out of scope.** Re-voice the
  body prose only. Leave the mermaid/typst blocks, the bold rule statements, and
  the chapter synopsis structure as they are unless asked otherwise.
